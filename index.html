Perfect Prediction Equilibrium

An alternative to the Nash Equilibrium

View project on GitHub

The Nash equilibrium is well established today

Different people behave in different ways when they make decisions and interact with their peers. Non-cooperative game theory, in its modern state, began roughly sixty years ago when John Nash formalized the behavior of rational players and described the equilibrium they reach. Yet, Nash Equilibria do not account for all real life situations.

Two rationalities

Newcomb's problem shows that people do not necessarily agree on what is rational to do. This problem points to two kinds of rationalities, Nash being one of them -- those who take two boxes. The PPE accounts for the rest of us who take only one box, on the same class of games (extensive form, perfect information, no random moves).

The papers

Several papers have been published or put online. The paper giving the philosophical foundations of the PPE framework dates back to 2000 and conjectures the existence, uniqueness and Pareto-optimality of the PPE. The most recent paper confirms and proves this conjecture, and gives the complete game-theoretical and algorithmic description of the PPE. It is currently available on arxiv.org and is being submitted to a journal.

Honesty is worth it

One of the nice take-away messages of the PPE is that it accounts for the behavior of honest people, yet in a non-cooperative framework. In particular, it leads to the optimal outcome of the promise game, in which Peter trusts Mary and Mary fulfils her promise. More general, the outcome of the PPE is always Pareto-optimal amongst the outcomes of the game.
